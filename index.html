Analysis of Noise in Diffusion Models for Image Synthesis

Austin Hartley
CPSC 8810-ML-based Image Synthesis, Clemson University
Paper Data

Diffuson Model: The diffusion model used is the time-varying noise model from the paper "Blue noise for diffusion models" by Huang et al. This model starts off at pure uncorrelated Gaussian white noise and slowly blends into a chosen noise over time through linear interpolation.

Abstract

In the field of image synthesis, diffusion models have been proven to create high-quality images. This created a surge of research attempting to improve the training process. Most of these new studies have solely used uncorrelated Gaussian white noise at all points in training, leaving the effects of correlated noise under-researched. This paper analyzes the state-of-the-art time-varying diffusion model's use of blue noise and expands upon their research to add more insights to the relationship between the denoising process of diffusion models and the type of noise used on image results.

The Backward Process

The backward process goes from low frequency (structure) to high frequency (finer details). The time-varying noise model proves that emphasizing the high frequency components can increase image quality. In this paper, experiments are conducted to see how important emphasizing the low frequency components at the beginning is in terms of image quality and if types of noise can be leveraged in other ways to produce certain stylistic attributes, such as more unique cat fur patterns.


Experiments

The experiments to test the importance of emphasizing the low frequencies at the start used red to white for a duration and then switched to white to blue. This was done at 20% to 80%, 50% to 50%, and 80% to 20%. To further add to that evidence, a blend of red and blue was then blended with white noise, where red noise was emphasized more heavily in the beginning and blue noise in the end, once with a linear blend and once with a quadraitc blend. Tests were also done using perlin noise with the same structure as the white to blue.


Results

The results showed that overly emphasizing the lower frequencies would lead to worse results, and a smaller emphasis for a smaller duration is better. However, those results were still worse than the white to blue blend, due to the blending causing the training to become more difficult. This implies further tailoring of the blend and noise scheduler would be required to acheive better results than the white to blue blend. Blending noises together is also not a straightfoward process, as it can easily produce artifacts and worse results. There is also a case to be made that inserting specific types of noise in certain areas could allow for new stylistic attributes.



Results- Images

The experiments of this paper are compared to the uncorrelated Gaussian white noise and the white to blue noise produced by the time-varying noise model.
